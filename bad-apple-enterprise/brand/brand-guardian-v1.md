## PREAMBLE

This document is not a mood board. It is a fence. Everything inside the fence is Bad Apple. Everything outside it is not. Your job is to know the difference before you post, print, ship, or sign anything.

Bad Apple is a Jamaican-born global brand built on two foundations: financial discipline (the trading bots) and cultural identity (the streetwear). Neither exists without the other. The clothing is not a side project. The bots are not just software. Together they form one ecosystem with one voice, two characters, and zero tolerance for dilution.

When in doubt, come back to this document. If the document doesn't cover it, escalate before you publish.

## SECTION 1: VOICE AND TONE GUIDE

### What Bad Apple Sounds Like

Bad Apple speaks from a position of earned credibility. Not hype. Not hustle culture posturing. Not tourist-facing Jamaican aesthetics. The brand voice is built on three foundations:

**Grounded** — every statement is rooted in something real: real heritage, real financial discipline, real craft. No empty inspiration.

**Precise** — Bad Apple does not ramble. Every caption, every product description, every CTA is cut down to what needs to be said. Silence is respected. Brevity is power.

**Ancestral** — the brand carries the weight of where it comes from. Jamaica is not a costume. References to yard culture, patois, madras, headwraps, and the grind are used because they are true, not because they test well with an audience.

---

### Vocabulary: What Bad Apple Uses

These words and phrases are native to the brand. Use them without explanation or apology.

- Built in the dark
- Yard to World
- The grind is private
- Precision
- The build
- Root system
- Legacy, not clout
- Dressed for the work
- Bad Apple. On purpose.
- Chakka / Kwasheba (always spelled exactly this way)
- Member / membership (not "customer" or "user")
- Drop (not "launch" or "release")
- Authentic, not tourist-facing
- From Seeds to Success
- The ecosystem
- 17BA

---

### What Bad Apple Never Says

These phrases are off-brand. Do not use them under any circumstances.

- "Vibe" (unearned, weightless)
- "Fire" as an adjective (social media slang with no shelf life)
- "Drip" (generic streetwear vocabulary, not specific to us)
- "Limited edition" without membership context (we do limited drops, but the word "limited" alone is a fast-fashion cliche)
- "Inspired by Jamaican culture" (Jamaica is not inspiration — it is origin)
- "Available for everyone" (the membership mechanic means access is earned)
- "Luxury" without qualification (we are not a luxury brand — we are a precision brand)
- "Hype" or "hypebeast" (not our world)
- Any reference to other streetwear brands in comparison ("like Supreme but...")
- "Invest in yourself" (too generic, too used)
- "Reggae-inspired" (reductive and tourist-facing)
- "Exotic" in reference to any Jamaican element

---

### 5 Copy Examples That Sound Wrong

**Wrong 1:**
"These fire fits just dropped! Get your drip on with our new Jamaican-inspired collection!"
— Sounds like a fast-fashion brand using Jamaica as a trend. Never.

**Wrong 2:**
"Limited edition! Don't sleep or you'll miss out!"
— Urgency through scarcity panic is not how Bad Apple operates. Members have access. That is the mechanism. FOMO marketing is beneath the brand.

**Wrong 3:**
"Invest in yourself — look good, feel good, do good."
— Empty. Generic. Could be any wellness or fashion brand. Has no Bad Apple DNA.

**Wrong 4:**
"Chakka and Kwasheba teamed up for this special collab piece — featuring the best of both!"
— Chakka and Kwasheba do not cross lanes. This violates the character canon. See Section 3.

**Wrong 5:**
"Inspired by the vibrant culture and reggae rhythms of Jamaica, our new collection brings island vibes to your wardrobe."
— Every word of this is wrong. "Inspired by," "vibrant," "reggae rhythms," "island vibes" — this is tourist-facing language. It treats Jamaica as a mood. Jamaica is the root system, not the vibe.

## SECTION 2: VISUAL IDENTITY RULES

### What Always Appears

Every piece of visual content must contain at least one of the following anchors. Without an anchor, it is not Bad Apple content.

1. The 17BA wordmark or logo (primary identifier)
2. A Chakka or Kwasheba visual element — never both in the same frame (see Section 3)
3. A color palette from the correct character lane (no mixing)
4. One brand pillar phrase used as a typographic element, if text is present
5. Madras/bandana pattern used correctly — as structural texture, never as a pop-culture reference

---

### What Never Appears

These are absolute prohibitions. No exceptions, no creative interpretations.

- Chakka palette colors (Charcoal, Black, Gold, Olive, Red) applied to Kwasheba content, or vice versa
- Rasta color combinations (Red, Gold, Green together) — these belong to Rastafari, not to Bad Apple. Using them would be appropriation and misrepresentation
- Generic tropical imagery (palm trees, waves, sunsets) presented as representative of Jamaica
- Stereotyped Jamaican imagery (dreadlocks as decoration, ganja leaves as pattern, "No Problem Mon" typography)
- Any honey badger illustration that is cute, friendly, or non-aggressive — both characters carry intensity, not cuteness
- Stock photography of Jamaican landmarks used as background filler
- Gradients in brand content (the palette is flat and precise — gradients are soft, Bad Apple is not)
- Comic Sans, Papyrus, or any font with Caribbean tourist-market associations
- Mixing Chakka and Kwasheba in the same visual frame (see Section 3 for full rules)
- The word "Jamaica" in a font style that mimics reggae poster lettering

---

### Chakka Visual Lane

Chakka's visual language is the trading terminal meets the yard. Every design element must be legible as either financial data structure or Jamaican heritage — ideally both simultaneously.

**Mandatory visual elements for Chakka content:**
- Palette: Charcoal (#2B2B2B or equivalent), Black (#0A0A0A), Gold (#C9A84C), Olive (#4A4E35), Red (#8B1A1A or equivalent). These five. No additions.
- Candlestick patterns used as actual design motifs — not as decorative suggestion. The chart is the pattern.
- Grid ladder structures — horizontal and vertical rule systems that reference both trading grids and Jamaican architectural grill work
- Resistance and support lines used as stitching detail references, border elements, or typography underlines
- Madras/bandana cloth patterns in Charcoal and Gold colorways only — never the traditional multi-color madras for Chakka
- Typography is heavy, geometric, and masculine — no script fonts in Chakka content

**Photography and styling rules for Chakka:**
- Models or subjects are active, purposeful, never posed for fashion's sake
- Settings: industrial, raw, urban, or overtly financial (trading setups, market environments)
- Lighting: controlled, dramatic, low-key. Nothing soft or editorial.
- No smiling at camera. The work is serious.

---

### Kwasheba Visual Lane

Kwasheba's visual language is heritage femininity meets financial precision. Softness is earned through structure, not decoration.

**Mandatory visual elements for Kwasheba content:**
- Palette: Cream (#F5EDD6), Terra/Rust (#8B4513), Burgundy (#6B1C2C), Gold (#C9A84C), Sage (#6B7C5C), Stone (#9B9B8B or equivalent). These six. No additions.
- Headwrap silhouettes used as structural design elements — the wrap form appears in collar construction, sleeve detail, and print motifs
- Traditional multi-color madras cloth in Cream, Terra, and Burgundy colorways — this is where the full madras palette lives
- Fibonacci spiral as a recurring structural motif in stitching, seam placement, button arrangement, and graphic elements
- Patois typography used selectively and correctly (see Heritage Layer Rules below)
- Script fonts are permitted but must be clean, structured, and legible — not decorative or whimsical

**Photography and styling rules for Kwasheba:**
- Subjects carry authority — standing still has power, movement is deliberate
- Settings: domestic spaces with dignity (not poverty aesthetics), natural outdoor light, or studio with warm neutral backgrounds
- Lighting: warm, golden hour reference, but structured — not soft-focus
- The headwrap is optional in photography but when present it is always treated as the primary design element, never as an afterthought

---

### Heritage Layer Rules

**Madras/Bandana Cloth**

The madras pattern is a real textile with real Jamaican cultural significance. It is not a pattern library you pull from. Apply these rules precisely.

- Madras appears as a structural texture element, not an all-over print unless the garment is specifically a madras-cloth construction
- When used in digital content, the madras reference must be accurate to actual Jamaican madras — the plaid structure, the color relationships, the scale. Do not approximate it with a generic tartan or plaid
- Chakka madras: Charcoal and Gold on Black ground. Single color variation only.
- Kwasheba madras: Traditional multi-color madras in the Kwasheba palette. The pattern lives here.
- Madras is never used as a background fill in marketing materials. It is a feature, not a wallpaper.

**Patois Text**

Patois is a language. It is not a font style or a creative choice. Apply it with the following rules.

- Patois text is used only in Kwasheba content (the heritage-feminine character lane carries the language legacy)
- Any patois phrase must be accurate to the language — if a team member is not a native speaker, the phrase must be reviewed by someone who is before it goes to production
- Patois text is used in select touchpoints: inside collar tags, embroidery detail, NFC-tag authentication screens, and select campaign copy. It is not used in every post.
- Never translate patois in the same frame. If you explain it, it loses its function. Trust the audience.
- Patois typography is set in clean, legible typefaces. The language carries the culture. The font does not need to perform it.
- Patois phrases that are common enough to have been commercialized elsewhere ("No Problem Mon," "Irie," "Ya Mon") are permanently off-limits. They are tourist territory.

## SECTION 3: CHARACTER CANON RULES

### The Separation Principle

Chakka and Kwasheba are not a duo. They are not a couple. They are not complementary halves that meet in the middle. They are two distinct characters with two distinct design systems who exist in the same ecosystem but occupy separate lanes.

This is not a creative constraint. It is the architecture of the brand.

**The rule is simple: Chakka and Kwasheba never appear in the same visual frame, the same product, or the same campaign.**

The only exception is the 17BA masterbrand level — ecosystem communications where both characters are referenced as part of the larger brand story. Even here, they are listed separately, not shown together.

---

### Chakka Canon

**Who Chakka is:**
A male honey badger who built himself in the dark. He is not celebrated. He is not validated by others. He has been grinding before anyone was watching and will keep grinding after they stop. He is associated with the Chakka EA trading bot — the aggressive, data-driven, solitary algorithm.

**What Chakka does:**
- Operates alone. The grind is private.
- References the chart, the data, the market — these are his language
- Occupies industrial, urban, or raw environments
- Speaks in declarative statements. No questions. No uncertainty.
- Embodies the men's collection completely — every men's piece is Chakka's domain

**What Chakka does not do:**
- Does not smile, wave, or perform friendliness in brand imagery
- Does not appear soft, vulnerable, or emotional in any marketing context
- Does not appear in Kwasheba color palettes under any circumstances
- Does not appear in content that references the women's collection or Kwasheba's design elements
- Does not speak in patois (patois is Kwasheba's heritage language layer)
- Does not reference Fibonacci or headwrap motifs — those belong to Kwasheba's lane

**Chakka's design system elements (belonging exclusively to him):**
- Candlestick pattern motifs
- Grid ladder structures
- Resistance and support line graphics
- Charcoal, Black, Gold, Olive, Red palette
- Heavy geometric typography
- Trading terminal visual references

---

### Kwasheba Canon

**Who Kwasheba is:**
A female honey badger who carries legacy. She is disciplined because she understands what she is building and who comes after her. She is associated with the Kwasheba EA trading bot — the methodical, spiral-structured, heritage-informed algorithm. She does not rush. She calculates.

**What Kwasheba does:**
- Builds with generational awareness — the decisions she makes account for the future
- References heritage, structure, and precision as intertwined values
- Occupies warm, authoritative spaces — she is not in the industrial grind, she is in the place where the results of the grind manifest
- Speaks with weight and selectivity — she says less than Chakka but means more
- Embodies the women's collection completely — every women's piece is Kwasheba's domain

**What Kwasheba does not do:**
- Does not appear aggressive or combative in the way Chakka does — her power is architectural, not confrontational
- Does not appear in Chakka color palettes under any circumstances
- Does not appear in content that references trading charts, grid ladders, or resistance lines — those belong to Chakka's lane
- Does not appear girlish, cute, or soft in a way that undermines her authority
- Does not appear as supporting character to Chakka, or vice versa — she is not his counterpart. She is independent.

**Kwasheba's design system elements (belonging exclusively to her):**
- Headwrap silhouette motifs
- Fibonacci spiral structures
- Traditional multi-color madras in the Kwasheba palette
- Patois typography and language
- Cream, Terra/Rust, Burgundy, Gold, Sage, Stone palette
- Structured script typography (when used)

---

### Both Characters — Shared Character Rules

- Neither character is ever depicted as defeated, weak, or humiliated — even in humor
- Neither character endorses products or brands outside the 17BA ecosystem without full Brand Guardian review and explicit approval
- Neither character is used in meme formats that undermine their design integrity
- Neither character speaks in a voice that contradicts their established personality
- Both characters are honey badgers — their animal design must carry the known characteristics of the honey badger (fearless, solitary, impossible to intimidate). Any illustration or 3D rendering must preserve these traits.
- Neither character is ever rendered in a cute, cartoonish, or child-friendly aesthetic. This is an adult brand.

## SECTION 4: CONTENT FILTER

### Pre-Publication Brand Compliance Checklist

Every piece of content — post, story, email, product description, hang tag, NFC screen, campaign visual, or collaboration asset — must clear this checklist before it is published, sent, or produced. This is not optional.

**IDENTITY CHECK**

- [ ] Does this content carry a 17BA brand anchor (wordmark, character element, or brand pillar phrase)?
- [ ] Is the correct character clearly identified — either Chakka (men's/bot-aggressive) or Kwasheba (women's/bot-disciplined) — but not both in the same frame?
- [ ] If this is a product piece, does it belong to one character's lane and stay entirely within that lane?

**PALETTE CHECK**

- [ ] If this is Chakka content, are only Chakka palette colors present (Charcoal, Black, Gold, Olive, Red)?
- [ ] If this is Kwasheba content, are only Kwasheba palette colors present (Cream, Terra/Rust, Burgundy, Gold, Sage, Stone)?
- [ ] Are gradients absent from this content?
- [ ] Are Rasta color combinations (Red, Gold, Green together) absent from this content?

**VOICE CHECK**

- [ ] Does the copy sound like it was written by someone who earned their position, not someone performing hustle culture?
- [ ] Is the copy free of the prohibited phrases listed in Section 1?
- [ ] If patois is used, has a native or fluent speaker reviewed it for accuracy?
- [ ] If patois is used, is it appearing in Kwasheba content only?
- [ ] Is Jamaica referenced as origin and root system, not as aesthetic inspiration or cultural trend?

**HERITAGE CHECK**

- [ ] If madras pattern is used, is it applied as a structural element (not wallpaper)?
- [ ] If madras is used in Chakka content, is it in Charcoal and Gold on Black only?
- [ ] If madras is used in Kwasheba content, is it the traditional multi-color format in the Kwasheba palette?
- [ ] Is all Jamaican cultural reference specific and accurate — not generic, not stereotyped?

**MEMBERSHIP MECHANIC CHECK**

- [ ] If this content references access to a drop, does it correctly reflect the membership mechanic (bot purchase unlocks drop)?
- [ ] Does this content avoid implying open access to membership-gated drops?
- [ ] If NFC/QR authentication is referenced, is it described accurately?

**CHARACTER INTEGRITY CHECK**

- [ ] If Chakka appears, is he depicted as solitary, precise, and non-friendly in his brand posture?
- [ ] If Kwasheba appears, is she depicted as authoritative, heritage-grounded, and architecturally powerful?
- [ ] Are both characters absent from any cute, cartoonish, or child-friendly rendering?
- [ ] Are the characters absent from meme formats or content that undermines their design integrity?

**PARTNERSHIP AND COLLABORATION CHECK (if applicable)**

- [ ] Has this collaboration been reviewed against the five alignment criteria in Section 5?
- [ ] Has this collaboration been cleared of the three automatic disqualifiers in Section 5?
- [ ] Is the partner brand credited in a way that does not elevate them above or equal to Bad Apple in the content hierarchy?

If any box is unchecked and you cannot resolve it yourself, the content is held. Escalate to the Brand Guardian before proceeding.

## SECTION 5: COLLABORATION AND PARTNERSHIP CRITERIA

### The Five Things a Partner Must Align With

All five must be present. Four out of five is not enough.

**1. Craft Discipline**
The partner must be known for making something with precision and intention. Fast-fashion volume brands, trend-chasing streetwear labels, and generic lifestyle companies do not meet this criterion regardless of their follower count or revenue.

**2. Cultural Rootedness**
The partner must have a genuine origin story connected to a specific place, community, or practice. The principle that applies to Bad Apple — heritage is origin, not inspiration — must be detectable in their work as well.

**3. Ecosystem Compatibility**
The collaboration must make sense within the 17BA ecosystem. Collaborations that exist purely for social reach with no ecosystem logic are rejected.

**4. Character Lane Clarity**
The partner must be able to identify which character their collaboration aligns with — Chakka or Kwasheba — and must be capable of respecting the lane separation. If a partner proposes content or product that would require mixing the two character systems, they are not ready to collaborate with Bad Apple.

**5. Audience Trust**
The partner must have built an audience through demonstrated value delivery — not through controversy, trend participation, or manufactured virality.

---

### Three Automatic Disqualifiers

**Disqualifier 1: Appropriation of Jamaican Culture as Aesthetic**
Any brand that has used Jamaican culture, imagery, language, or music as a trend element without Jamaican creative leadership is disqualified.

**Disqualifier 2: Contradiction of the Membership Mechanic Logic**
Any brand whose business model depends on open access, mass-market availability, or deliberate democratization of access is disqualified.

**Disqualifier 3: Character or Values Misalignment on Record**
Any brand, individual, or organization that has publicly demonstrated values that contradict Bad Apple's brand pillars is disqualified where a documented public record exists.

---

### Partnership Process Note

When a collaboration is approved:
- Bad Apple's brand guidelines govern all shared creative. The partner adapts to us.
- All collaboration content clears the Section 4 checklist before publication
- The collaboration is anchored to one character lane — Chakka or Kwasheba. Mixed-character collab content is not produced under any circumstances.
- The 17BA wordmark takes primary position in all shared branding.
